## Introduction

The songstress, or singing actress, became a key figure that linked the industries of film, music, and popular press in 1930s China. These entertainment industries formed what Andrew Jones has called a "media circuit," a circuit in which songs featured in films became popular as records, records that in turn promoted the cinema. All the while, the audiovisual industries were supported by a vibrant print culture that disseminated text, image, and musical notation in the service of selling even more music records and movie tickets.

The film is a star vehicle for Zhou Xuan <span lang="zh">周璇</span> (1920-1957), the singer known as the "Golden Voice" (jin sangzi <span lang="zh">金嗓子</span>) and one of the most popular songstresses of the "golden age" of Chinese cinema in the 1930s. Zhou's career began with the musician and producer Li Jinhui's <span lang="zh">李锦晖</span> (1891-1967) Bright Moon Song and Dance Troupe <span lang="zh">明月歌舞團</span>, an institution that produced most of the period's popular singers, musicians, and actors. Working closely with film studios in Shanghai, Li pioneered the "hybrid genre" of shidai qu <span lang="zh">时代曲</span> that combined "American jazz, Hollywood film music, and Chinese folk music." Literally dubbed "modern songs," shidai qu are also known as "yellow music" (huangse yinyue <span lang="zh">黄色音樂</span>), a pejorative term used by Chinese modernizing reformers and the socialist cultural establishment to underscore the genre's suspect ties to a decadent commercial culture.

The 1940 Western Wing contributed an enduring musical artefact to this media circuit: the romantic shidai qu "A Full Moon and Beautiful Flowers" <span lang="zh">月圓花好</span> composed by celebrated writer and lyricist Fan Yanqiao <span lang="zh">范烟橋</span> (1894-1967). Originally sung by Zhou Xuan's Hongniang in the film, the song was concurrently issued as a 78 shellac record by Pathé <span lang="zh">上海百代公司</span>, one of Shanghai's biggest record companies. It quickly became a hit, as did two later collaborations between Zhou and Fan, "The Blooming Years" <span lang="zh">花样的年華</span> and "Shanghai Nights" <span lang="zh">夜上海</span>, both equally iconic Shanghai jazz songs.

## Plot Summary

The 1940 film adaptation begins in the temple, where Scholar Zhang <span lang="zh">張生</span> has just arrived and is being shown the grounds. On his tour, he notices Cui Yingying <span lang="zh">崔鶯鶯</span> and her maid Hongniang <span lang="zh">紅娘</span> and is immediately smitten with the young lady. He arranges to see her again by joining a temple ceremony. Later, the two exchange poems across a garden wall, further confirming their growing attraction. When the bandit Sun Feihu <span lang="zh">孫飛虎</span> demands that Yingying marry him, Student Zhang devises a strategy to save her by enlisting the help of his friend General White Horse <span lang="zh">白馬将軍</span>. He then expects Yingying's mother to make good on her promise to wed her daughter to anyone who saved her from the bandit. Madam Cui, however, reneges on her promise. Student Zhang is devastated and falls ill, recovering only when he realizes that Yingying does return his affections. With Hongniang's help, the two consummate their romance, but are quickly found out by Madam Cui. She finally agrees to the match but demands that Student Zhang pass the imperial exams before they marry. After he goes off, however, she again betrays Student Zhang and plans a more advantageous match. Luckily, Student Zhang, having successfully passed his exams, returns in time to stop the wedding, and the lovers are happily reunited.

## Theme: Zhou Xuan's Hongniang, the Intermedial Songstress

The 1940 Western Wing film was a "mega production" (juzhi <span lang="zh">巨制</span>) that featured musical performance, elaborate sets, and rich costumes. This Western Wing represents a blend of the "opera film" (xiqu pian <span lang="zh">戲曲片</span>) and the "musical film" (gechang pian <span lang="zh">歌唱片</span>) genres. "Modern song" (shidai qu <span lang="zh">时代曲</span>) musical numbers are interjected into the narrative as they are in Hollywood musicals, popular in China throughout the 1930s, but characters also deliver lines of dialogue in song. Whenever characters break into song, the symbiotic relationship between cinema and the music industry is underscored visually: the title, lyricist, and composer of each song appears on screen. Such film songs are literally called "interjected songs" (chaqu <span lang="zh">插曲</span>) and function as moments of musical pleasure as well as narrative supplement.

The film's first chaqu is "The Butterfly Tune." The song's title and credits appear on screen just before Zhou Xuan, as Hongniang, prompts Yingying to look at the butterflies in the garden and begins singing. The song's lyrics appear as subtitles as she sings:

As she sings, the two women walk across an ornate bridge and enjoy watching the butterflies that flutter around them. When Hongniang reaches the middle of the song ("But butterflies get no marks for bearing fruit"), the film cuts to a doorway from which Student Zhang looks at the two women, stupefied. When Hongniang finishes her song, she realizes that the young man is staring at them and that Yingying is looking back at him.

Zhou Xuan's performance does a lot of work in this scene. She is foregrounded as a singer at the beginning of the story, a move that engages the audience through her celebrity status. While Student Zhang is clearly staring at Yingying, when he discusses the women with the monk Facong after the women leave, he first asks about the identity of the singer. Yingying may be lovely to look at, but Zhou Xuan's voice is given priority even in the dialogue. Within the film's diegesis, the song introduces Hongniang the maid as a crucial musical and visual mediator between the lovers. The butterflies in her song lyrics give us a first clue—like the butterflies that bring together pollen and flower, Hongniang's actions enable coupling.

Visually, the film likewise both relies on Hongniang and displaces her. She looks askance between Yingying and Student Zhang, becoming aware of the potentially scandalous chemistry between the two. This realization marks the moment as a significant one for the audience. It also occurs literally to the side of the two lovers' eyeline match, leaving Hongniang just outside the romance that her song seems to have initiated.

Hongniang's frustration and detachment is made most obvious in the song that became a hit record, "A Full Moon and Beautiful Flowers." This song, however, is conspicuously absent in the extant cut of the film. According to the script, a summary of which was published in a special issue of the film magazine Gold City Monthly <span lang="zh">金城月刊</span> concurrently with the film's release, Hongniang is supposed to sing this romantic ballad while Student Zhang and Yingying are sexually intimate with one another in his quarters.

In the extant film, Hongniang brings Yingying to Student Zhang's door. He pulls her into the room and then returns to the door to close it. Hongniang seems taken aback by the abruptly shut door and looks in through the lattice. She pouts that she's a dupe and walks away. The film then cuts to another scene. The script printed in the magazine, however, runs several more lines of poetic dialogue and then indicates that Hongniang sings the film's signature song.

Again, Hongniang's musical performance serves to both mediate the others' romance and highlight her own affective state. The romantic pleasures happening just beyond the door are displaced for the audience into the song's aural and poetic pleasures. Hongniang is not directly party to the lovers' romance, but the audience is invited to vicariously experience the love scene through her song. She is again both crucial to mediating romantic feelings (including her own desires) and detached from a romantic union.

The song's absence from the film may prevent us from seeing Zhou Xuan's performance, but it also prompts us to discover how film songs themselves could be detached from the cinema per se and circulate in other media. "Full Moon" was as much part of The Western Wing as it was a hit single that functioned more generally as a generic love song. It was available both as a record and circulated in musical notation published in film magazines and special collections of popular songs.

A video on the Chinese streaming site bilibili can give us a sense of listening to the record. Part of a global nostalgic trend that celebrates the materiality of the record, the clip is a long take of the actual 1940 record as it plays back Zhou Xuan's song. In addition to seeing the physical object, we hear the noise generated in the playback. The video insists on a visual and aural authenticity; seeing and hearing the wear and tear of the record produces an "aura" that is meant to affectively transport its viewers in time to the 1940s. Zhou Xuan's photograph, on the right side of the slip, provides further visual cues for the imagination.

Hongniang's lost song has likewise been resurrected by another nostalgic internet trend, namely, AI restoration. Various restored versions of Zhou Xuan's voice exists online. In addition to offering a crisp rendition of the original song, one version includes AI-colorized footage from the film, set to edited footage of the garden scene when Hongniang sings the "Butterfly Tune." The video even includes subtitles to the song's lyrics that replace the original lyrics visible in this scene with the new song. Though not copied exactly, the new font even echoes the handwritten characters original to the 1940s film. This scene is not a surprising choice of footage to accompany the iconic "Full Moon." As analyzed above, it is the moment in which the film introduces its star Zhou Xuan and inaugurates its romance between Yingying and Student Zhang. Now, however, Zhou Xuan's initial appearance and the lovers' initial meeting are set to the film's most famous song. The situation confirms Jean Ma's argument that the songstress "also challenges us to pay attention to those large-scale temporal connections and cross-regional transactions that transcend the boundaries of any individual milieu."
