## Introduction

Chuanju (<span lang="zh">川劇</span>, Sichuan opera) is one of the major regional opera traditions of China, with a history stretching back at least to the Ming dynasty (1368–1644) and a distinctive aesthetic shaped by Sichuan's geography, culture, and theatrical traditions. Chuanju is characterized by its use of five different musical systems — Kunqiang (<span lang="zh">崑腔</span>), Gaoqiang (<span lang="zh">高腔</span>), Huqin (<span lang="zh">胡琴</span>), Tanxi (<span lang="zh">彈戲</span>), and Dengdiao (<span lang="zh">燈調</span>) — of which Gaoqiang (the high-pitch style) is considered the most characteristic and the most technically demanding. Gaoqiang originated in the Yiyang musical tradition of Jiangxi province and was brought to Sichuan by traveling troupes; it is performed without orchestral accompaniment, relying instead on percussion instruments and a chorus of off-stage singers who amplify and comment on the lead performer's arias.

Chuanju is also known for its acrobatic performance style and its tradition of face-changing (<span lang="zh">变脸</span>, bian lian), in which performers rapidly change their masks as a way of expressing shifts in a character's emotional state. The tradition has produced remarkable performers over the centuries, and it continues to be a living art form, with active troupes in Chengdu and other cities in Sichuan province.

Xu Fen (<span lang="zh">徐棻</span>, b. 1935) is one of the most celebrated playwrights in the history of modern Chuanju. She began writing for the Sichuan opera stage in the 1950s and has continued to write and adapt plays across a career spanning more than six decades. Her work is notable for its literary quality, its attention to female characters, and its willingness to engage critically with the dramatic and social conventions of the tradition. Her adaptation of *A Clever Maid Maneuvers Her Way into Romance* as *Yanyan* (<span lang="zh">燕燕</span>) was first performed in the 1980s and has become one of the most celebrated works in her oeuvre.

## Plot Summary

Xu Fen's adaptation of Guan Hanqing's play significantly transforms the original while retaining its central character and its central dramatic situation. In Xu Fen's version, the young nobleman Li Weide (<span lang="zh">李維德</span>) is a more sympathetically drawn character than his counterpart in the Yuan zaju: he genuinely cares for Yanyan and is genuinely torn between his feelings for her and the social obligations that make their union impossible.

The character of Yingying (<span lang="zh">莺莺</span>) — the noblewoman whom Li Weide's family intends him to marry — is also more complex in Xu Fen's version than in the original. Yingying is not merely a social obstacle; she is aware of Yanyan's feelings for Li Weide, and she uses this knowledge in ways that are sometimes sympathetic and sometimes calculated. Her character adds a layer of psychological complexity to the play that is largely absent from the more schematically drawn figures of the Yuan original.

The most significant departure from the original is the ending. In Guan Hanqing's play, the resolution of Yanyan's situation is ambiguous but ultimately survivable: she achieves a form of recognition and a secured place in Li Weide's household. In Xu Fen's adaptation, the ending is far more radical: Yanyan, faced with the prospect of being forced into a marriage she does not want, chooses death over submission. Her suicide in the nuptial bed is an act of defiance — a rejection of the social order that denies her the right to choose her own fate — and it is the emotional and thematic climax of the production.

This radical ending was influenced, Xu Fen has suggested, by the social and cultural context of its creation. Written and first performed in a period when Chinese society was grappling with the legacy of the Cultural Revolution and beginning to engage with questions of individual rights and personal autonomy, the play's ending resonated with contemporary debates about the relationship between the individual and the collective, between personal desire and social obligation.

## Theme: The Physicality of Emotions on the Opera Stage

Wang Yumei (<span lang="zh">王玉梅</span>) is one of the most celebrated Chuanju performers of her generation. She received the Plum Performance Award (<span lang="zh">梅花奖</span>, the highest honor in Chinese opera performance) for her portrayal of Yanyan, and her performance in this role is widely regarded as a landmark in the tradition. Her interpretation of the character is notable above all for its physicality: the way she uses her body, her voice, and the props available to her on the Chuanju stage to give concrete, visible form to emotional states that in other theatrical traditions might be conveyed primarily through dialogue or song.

The spatiality of emotions — the idea that emotional states can be mapped onto spatial relationships, body positions, and physical gestures — is central to the aesthetics of Chinese opera. In Chuanju, as in other regional opera traditions, performers undergo years of training to master a codified vocabulary of physical expression: particular ways of moving the hands, the eyes, the feet, and the body that communicate specific emotional states to an audience familiar with the codes. Wang Yumei's performance in *Yanyan* both draws on this codified vocabulary and extends it, finding ways to express the specific emotional texture of Yanyan's situation — her defiance, her longing, her grief, and her ultimate decision — through physical choices that are at once conventional and freshly felt.

### Clip 1: Rather Die than Get Married — A Defiant Maid Rejecting Social Norms

In this scene, the Lady of the House (<span lang="zh">夫人</span>) presents Yanyan with what she frames as a generous offer: marriage to a man named Wang Si (<span lang="zh">王四</span>), a match that would, in the Lady's view, resolve Yanyan's situation and provide her with a secure future. Yanyan rejects the offer with a vehemence that startles the Lady and establishes the terms of the conflict that will drive the rest of the play.

The physical gesture that crystallizes Yanyan's defiance is one of the most memorable in the production: she takes the cane that the Lady has set down — a prop associated with the authority of the household — and places it across her own neck, miming her willingness to die rather than comply with the proposed marriage. The gesture is bold, transgressive, and deeply theatrical; it transforms an object associated with social authority into an instrument of individual defiance. As shown in Fig. 1, the image of Yanyan with the cane over her head captures the character at her most physically expressive: upright, determined, and entirely unintimidated.

### Clip 2: A Heart in Turmoil — Yanyan's Romantic Passions

In this scene, Yanyan's emotional state is conveyed through her relationship with two props: a quilt and a lamp. The quilt — soft, intimate, associated with sleep and the private self — becomes a vehicle for expressing the turmoil of romantic feeling; the lamp — small, flickering, vulnerable to extinction — becomes an image of the fragility of hope and desire.

The music that accompanies this scene draws on the Gaoqiang tradition's characteristic way of guiding the performer and the audience through an emotional journey. The vocal line is elaborately ornamented, with long melismatic passages that allow the performer to dwell on particular syllables and thereby communicate the weight and texture of the emotions they carry. The lyrics draw on classical imagery associated with romantic longing: the willow tree, whose trailing branches suggest a reaching out toward the beloved; the lotus flower, which rises from muddy water to bloom in pristine beauty, suggesting the possibility of purity and grace even in inauspicious circumstances.

### Clip 3: Fear and Worry — Seduction

This scene is driven by percussion. The stage fills with the sound of drums and cymbals as the dramatic tension escalates: Yanyan is being pursued, and the insistent rhythmic drive of the percussion conveys both the physical urgency of the chase and the emotional turbulence of a character who is simultaneously attracted and frightened, who wants to run and wants to stay.

Yanyan's evasion of her pursuer is expressed through a sequence of rapid, precisely choreographed movements that draw on the Chuanju tradition's acrobatic vocabulary. She moves across the stage in arcs and spirals, using the space to express a psychological state of conflict and confusion. Her apron — a garment associated with her servant status — becomes a symbol in this scene: she clutches it, releases it, and clutches it again in a series of gestures that suggest the difficulty of letting go of the social role that both constrains and defines her.

The scene culminates in a moment of stillness: after the frantic movement of the chase, Yanyan stands alone on stage as the sound of distant thunder fills the auditorium. Her robes settle around her, and her stillness is both a physical relief and an emotional marker — a pause in which the full weight of her situation becomes visible.

### Clip 4: Pain and Anger — Heartbreak

The stage turns dark. In this scene, the lighting shift signals a corresponding shift in Yanyan's emotional state: from the turbulent conflict of the chase to the cold, still aftermath of heartbreak. Yanyan has learned that Li Weide will not — or cannot — choose her over the social obligations that bind him, and her response is a complex mixture of anger, grief, and wounded pride.

The anger is expressed through fast-paced lyrics delivered in a rapid, percussive vocal style that contrasts sharply with the long, ornamented lines of the romantic scenes. The words come out in quick bursts, each one a dart aimed at the situation that has brought her to this point. But the anger is underlaid by grief, and Wang Yumei finds ways to let the grief show through the anger: a tremor in the voice here, a momentary collapse of the body's uprightness there, before she pulls herself back into defiance.

The physical pain of heartbreak is rendered with particular specificity. Wang Yumei does not merely mime generic emotional distress; she locates the pain in specific parts of the body — the chest, the hands, the throat — and traces its movement through her body in a way that makes the invisible visible.

### Clip 5: Unspeakable Sadness — The Symbol of the Moth

In Xu Fen's adaptation, the image of a moth drawn to a candle flame becomes the central symbol of Yanyan's final emotional state. The moth cannot help flying toward the light; the light will destroy it; it flies toward the light anyway. The image is an ancient one in Chinese poetry, and its invocation here connects Yanyan's situation to a long tradition of writing about the irresistible pull of love and the annihilation it threatens.

This scene represents a significant departure from the original zaju, in which the emotional register is considerably less extreme. Where Guan Hanqing's Swallow is clever and spirited to the end, Xu Fen's Yanyan reaches a point of such profound sadness that words and rapid movement can no longer contain it. Wang Yumei's singing slows to a pace that allows each note to resonate; her body loses the upright, energized quality that has characterized her performance throughout and begins to curve inward, as if something essential has been withdrawn. She has difficulty standing — the physical effort of remaining upright becomes visible, and the struggle to maintain even that minimal posture is itself an expression of a will that is close to its end.

### Clip 6: Yanyan's Ultimate Defiance — Suicide

</figure>

The final scene of the production is structured around a deliberate contrast between the comic and the tragic. Wang Si (<span lang="zh">王四</span>), the man Yanyan has been ordered to marry, provides a last moment of comic relief: his oblivious cheerfulness as he prepares for the wedding night is the more poignant for its contrast with what the audience knows is coming.

The mise-en-scène of the suicide scene is unconventional by the standards of the Chuanju tradition. The nuptial bed — an object associated with joy, new beginnings, and the consummation of socially sanctioned union — becomes the site of Yanyan's death. She chooses to die in the very place where she is supposed to begin her new life as Wang Si's wife, and this choice transforms the most intimate domestic space into an arena of resistance.

The stage lighting shifts as Yanyan dies: from the warm red associated with wedding celebrations to a cold, dark tone that signals the end of the play's world of social obligation and surfaces the tragic dimension that has been present throughout. In the final image, Yanyan's expression is not one of defeat; it is one of defiance, of a will that has remained intact even as the body that housed it has been destroyed.

Mei Hua (<span lang="zh">梅花</span>), Yingying's servant, weeps at the side of the stage. Her grief is the production's acknowledgment that Yanyan's death is a loss — for the other characters, for the world of the play, and for the audience. The weeping of a minor character who had no power to prevent the tragedy is a classical dramatic device, one that gives form to the sorrow that the audience is intended to feel.
