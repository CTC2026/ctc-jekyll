## General Background

Female entertainers were prominent participants in the cultural life of Chinese courts from the earliest times onward. By the Tang dynasty (618–907), a well-established system of government-registered female entertainers existed throughout the empire. Women in this system were trained in music, dance, and poetry, and they served at banquets and official functions alongside the men who governed China's vast bureaucratic state. Over time, a thriving commercial pleasure quarter emerged in China's growing cities, and independent courtesans — women who sold their company, their art, and sometimes their bodies — became a fixture of urban life. By the Song dynasty (960–1279), the pleasure quarters of cities like Kaifeng and Hangzhou were famous for their brilliant entertainers, and the culture of the courtesan house had become deeply intertwined with the literary and artistic life of the educated elite.

During the Yuan dynasty (1271–1368), when Guan Hanqing (c. 1230–c. 1300) wrote his plays, the status of female entertainers was fixed in law. Government policy classified people into hereditary occupational categories, and the category of "entertainers" (yue 樂) was among the lowest. Women born into entertainer households were registered as such and could not freely leave the profession. The question of whether a courtesan could "ascend to commoner status" (cong liang 從良) — that is, leave the pleasure quarter by marrying a man of respectable standing — was one of the central preoccupations of Yuan drama, and it animates the plot of *Saving a Courtesan*.

Yuan zaju plays set in the pleasure quarters often drew on Buddhist tropes of compassion and salvation. The bodhisattva Guanyin, famous for manifesting in many forms to help those in need, was sometimes depicted as a courtesan who used her position to save souls. This imagery lent a spiritual dimension to the figure of the compassionate courtesan who sacrifices herself for others. Guan Hanqing's Zhao Pan'er belongs to this tradition: a woman of intelligence, moral courage, and deep loyalty who devises a daring plan to rescue her sworn sister from an abusive marriage.

The name "Pan'er" (盼兒, literally "one who hopes" or "one who longs") signals her character's orientation toward the future and toward the wellbeing of others. Pan'er is a former courtesan who has already managed to leave the pleasure quarter, likely through her own efforts, when the play begins. Her sworn sister Song Yinzhang (宋引章), a young musician still working in the pleasure quarter of Kaifeng, has rashly agreed to marry a man named Zhou She (周舍) against the advice of her elders. Pan'er suspects that Zhou She is a rake and a bully, and her suspicions prove correct: once Yinzhang is married and out of the pleasure quarter, Zhou She reveals himself to be cruel and abusive. The main conflict of the play turns on Pan'er's plan to rescue Yinzhang from this marriage.

Pan'er's rescue plan involves a bold act of deception. She travels to where Zhou She is living with Yinzhang, presents herself as a courtesan who has fallen in love with Zhou She, and uses her considerable skills of flattery and manipulation to convince him to divorce Yinzhang and marry her instead. Zhou She falls for the ruse, divorces Yinzhang, and then discovers — too late — that Pan'er has outsmarted him. The comedy of the play depends heavily on the physicality of Zhou She's humiliation; earlier skit traditions that fed into Yuan zaju drama were known for their broad physical comedy and slapstick altercations, and *Saving a Courtesan* carries traces of this heritage.

The play survives in two different editions that present different versions of the denouement. In one edition, Pan'er escapes with Yinzhang and is eventually reunited with her own lover, An Xiuqing (安秀卿), a scholar who has remained loyal to her. In the other edition, the ending is slightly different in its details, but the outcome is the same: Pan'er's cleverness triumphs, Yinzhang is freed, and the bond of sworn sisterhood is vindicated. The existence of two editions points to the fluid, performative nature of Yuan drama, which was a living theatrical tradition rather than a fixed literary text.

Modern adaptations of *Saving a Courtesan* face the challenging task of making this story legible to audiences unfamiliar with the Yuan dynasty pleasure quarter and its complex social codes. The two adaptations examined in this module — an English-language spoken play produced in London in 2021 and a Chinese television drama series broadcast in 2022 — each found distinctive strategies for translating the play's world and its central concerns into contemporary idioms.
